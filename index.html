Forgotten Posts: Who Is Watching the Watchers?
A correspondent's six-week attempt to visit a border installation raises questions nobody seems eager to answer
By Alden Rook  |  Staff Correspondent, Military Affairs

I have been trying, for six weeks now, to visit Fort Verano. This should not be a complicated matter.

I am a credentialed correspondent. Fort Verano is a functioning Amestrian military installation on Cretan soil, subject — in theory — to standard press access protocols under the 1899 Border Operations Agreement. I have filed three formal access requests. Two were denied without stated reason. One has not been answered at all.

"Institutions that have nothing to hide generally tell you what they're hiding it for."

I would not usually find this worth noting. Forward postings near contested borders have legitimate reasons to limit outside access. I have covered four comparable installations in the past two years. The paperwork was slow. It moved.

What I cannot account for is the response from the administrative contact listed on Fort Verano's official filing, who informed me the installation "does not currently fall under standard press access protocols" — a classification that exists nowhere in any agreement I have been able to locate.

I am also unable to account for two soldiers I attempted to contact — both of whom had written home about conditions at the fort recently. According to their families, both men have been transferred to postings that no available directory can locate. One family filed an inquiry three weeks ago. They are still waiting.

I want to be precise. I have no evidence of wrongdoing at Fort Verano. I have only a consistent pattern of questions being redirected or going unanswered.

But when an institution has nothing to hide, it tells you what it is hiding, and why.

This correspondent will continue to pursue access.

Rook covers military affairs for the Courier. Correspondence: 14 Vance St., East City

Military supply contracts — western border region
Three civilian logistics firms have been awarded short-term supply contracts for western border installations under a no-bid arrangement approved last quarter. The contracts, covering medical and chemical supply delivery, are listed under a discretionary procurement clause that does not require public tender. The firms involved have not been named in the public record. A parliamentary inquiry into the use of the discretionary clause was tabled in the spring session and has not been rescheduled.

Hundreds march on Governor's Office as anti-Ishvalan tensions mount in West City
"Send them back" — demonstrators demand expulsion of Ishvalan residents from western districts
An estimated four hundred demonstrators gathered outside the West City Governor's Office on the morning of the 12th, demanding what organisers called "the removal of Ishvalan squatters from Amestrian soil." The march, which began at Kandor Square and proceeded along the central boulevard, was organised by a coalition of groups that have grown in visibility since the close of the Ishval conflict three years ago.
Governor's staff confirmed that the building was placed under restricted access during the demonstration. No statement was issued. City constables maintained a perimeter but made no arrests. The march dispersed by midday.
Ishvalan community representatives declined to comment publicly. A neighbourhood liaison, speaking on condition of anonymity, said residents of the western district had "been expecting something like this for some time."
This is the third demonstration of this kind in West City in as many months. Previous gatherings drew smaller crowds. Organisers indicated a larger action is being planned.
